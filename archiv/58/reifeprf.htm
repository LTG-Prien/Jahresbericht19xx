Dienstag, den 16. Juni, von 8-8.30 Uhr

Englisches Diktat
(½ Stunde Arbeitszeit)

The long summer is over at last. This morning, a bluster of damp wind worried the branches overhead, and like fat brown hailstones a shower of acorns, many of them already splitting, fell from the old oak tree on the inhospitable asphalt below. Autumn has arrived, muddy as usual.

In the country the year can move into old age with natural grace, but in the suburbs of cities it is a nasty business. Leaves fall thick upon the pavements, a drizzle wets them, and the constant pounding of soles and heels makes a slimy porridge of them. Nowhere is the absurd idea of a marriage between country and town shown up more forcefully than in London suburbs during autumn: the melancholy misalliance is plainly exposed in streets where trees, growing through holes in asphalt or concrete, droop in dismal, ragged lines between monstrous housefronts of red or yellow brick.

There come to mind autumns in the country that were experienced with pleasure no less than that enjoyed in the tremulous airs of high summer or the sharp-scented breezes of spring. In the town autumn is simply a neither-nor: but in the country it exists firmly in its own right, it is a distinctive presence. This refers, not to the season of mellow fruitfulness, which is really an extension of summer, but to the period beyond it, in which the approaching death of the world is made manifest. Autumn unites past and future, summer and winter, life and death, in a present which is both tranquil and quick with excitement, and leads us to meditate the supreme omnipresent paradox, that to live it is first necessary to die.

 

Dienstag, den 16. Juni, von 8.40-10.00 Uhr

Englische Nacherzählung
(1 Stunde 20 Minuten Arbeitszeit)

The Wedding Present

People tell this story in England, and they swear it is true:
Two unmarried sisters, Miss Emily and Miss Margaret, lived in rather poor circumstances in a small village neighbouring the great estate belonging to the Duke of York, later King George VI.

When the Duke became engaged to marry, the sisters were greatly excited about it and decided to send a wedding present to their royal neighbour. So, armed with their savings of two pounds and a shilling, they set out in search of a suitable gift.

After walking about from shop to shop for many hours, they had almost given up the hope of finding a gift matching the occasion and, at the same time, not straining their purse too much. Finally, in a dusty, obscure shop window, they spied two beautiful chessmen - the king and the queen - and found the price to be just right.

Carefully they put them in a small box, which they mailed to the future Royal Couple. Then they settled down to wait for the "Royal Thank You" that was sure to come. It would be a souvenir to keep forever. When the long waited-for, unmistakable envelope arrived, it contained a message beyond their boldest dreams: an invitation to come to the Palace and to view the royal wedding gifts. They rode up to London, reached the Palace, were ceremoniously welcomed and shown in by servants in colourful uniforms. After taking a deep breath, they began to make their way through the countless rooms and halls in which the vast show of wedding gifts was displayed.

But they could not discover their humble chessmen anywhere. So, feeling a little sad and disappointed, they started to leave.

The way out led through a stately hall. And here, in splendid isolation, with a spotlight focused on them, they discovered their two chessmen on a costly stand. There was a gold-lettered sign saying that trough the great generosity of the Misses Emily and Margaret these invaluable pieces of Henry VIII's chess set had finally been found and restored to the royal house - and that the set was now complete again.

 

 

Dienstag, den 16. Juni, von 10.15-11.45 Uhr

Übersetzung aus dem Englischen in das Deutsche
(1 ½ Stunden Arbeitszeit)

Today all free-minded nations must get together and plan to make a good job of the world's affairs by concerted action because in essentials they have the same needs, demand justice in the same terms, and have the same respect for enlightened ideas. A certain community of thought and interest is presupposed, and if that were lacking we should revert to the barbarism of the Dark Ages.

So we have to build upon what we have in common without ever forgetting that each country has something all its own, deep-rooted in its history and tradition, unique, worth preserving - and worth sharing. Western Europe never became Greek, but it went to the ancient Greeks and drew deep draughts of their refreshing spirit. Britain and France never became Italian, but gained mental vigour from their studies of the Italian Renaissance. Britain, the Dominions, and the United States, already so near by reason of a common language and heritage of law and custom, have none the less qualities quite distinct, and therefore have much to learn from one another.

The basis of friendship is not identity of character but understanding and sympathy, and it deepens as each learns more about the other and from the other. Give and take there must be between nations as between friends; a desire to know more about the other's character and to benefit by the other's special experience. Since it is only by willing cooperation between nations that civilization can be saved and remade, there is perhaps nothing today that matters so much as that the nations which respect civilization should strive hard to understand and appreciate one another, each endeavouring to view the world both from its own standpoint and from that of all the rest.

From an editorial in "Britain To-day", No. 58, July 25, 1941.

Erster Rheinübergang

Germanico bello confecto multis de causis Caesar statuit sibi Rhenum esse transeundum; quarum illa fuit iustissima, quod, cum videret Germanos tam facile impelli, ut in Galliam venirent, suis quoque rebus eos timere voluit, eum intellegerent et posse et audere populi Romani exercitum Rhenum transire.

Sed navibus transire neque satis tutum esse arbitrabatur neque suae neque populi Romani dignitatis esse statuebat. Itaque, etsi summa difficultas faciendi pontis proponebatur propter latitudinem, rapiditatem altitudinemque fluminis, tamen id sibi contendendum aut aliter non traducendum exercitum existimabat.

Diebus decem, quibus materia coepta erat comportari, ohni opere perfecto exercitus traducitur. Caesar ad utramque partem pontis firmo praesidio relicto in fines Sugambrorum contendit. Interim a compluribus civitatibus ad eum legati veniunt; quibus pacem atque amicitiam petentibus liberaliter respondet obsidesque ad se adduei iubet. At Sugambri ex eo tempore, quo pons fieri coeptus est, fuga comparata hortantibus iis, quos ex Tencteris atque Usipetibus apud se habebant, finibus suis excesserant suaque omnia exportaverant seque in solitudinem ac silvas abdiderant.

(Caesar: De bello Gallico IV, 16/18)
