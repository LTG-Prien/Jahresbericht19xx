Donnerstag, den 19. Juni, von 8-8.30 Uhr
Englisches Diktat (½ Stunde Arbeitszeit)

Portrait of the U.S.A.

The major achievement of the Americans has been the integration within the boundaries of a single, unified nation of the infinite variety of racial, cultural, economic, and geographic components of the U. S. scene.

In the beginning there were pioneers who created colonies based on the ideal of self-sufficiency. Although they achieved a loose political unity when they were leagued under the Articles of Confederation, for all practical purposes they remained separate nations. Even under the Constitution the states placed their individual interests above the national interest, and this selfishness led to the Civil War. But a nation had been created, the frontier was pushed westward, and industry became a strong integrating force.

See the sweep, the magnitude of the achievementl On the map this giqantic slab of earth confronts the mind, the eye, and the imagination like a cake too big to eat . . . Here is the gentle loveliness of velvet lawns and flaming autumn hillsides in New England, and here the parched, harsh desolation of the prairies on a snowless day in winter. Here is the moss-hung lushness of palms tossing in the moonlight on the Louisiana shore, and here the austere march of evergreens up the western slopes of the Cascade Mountains. Here is New York at night, hell-red with neon and fogged by factories, and here is a lone rider herding sheep on an empty Wyoming plateau. Here is the whole land, laid lavishly across the belly of a continent, washed by three seas, wormed by a dozen suns, breathing a hundred airs, so vast that its horizons exhaust the eye, so turbulent with beauty, ugliness, terror, and hope that it wears a thousand faces and speaks with ten thousand tongues. All this has been integrated.

(Taken from: Wright and Swedenberg, The American Tradition, New York, 1943)


 

Donnerstag, den 19. Juni von 8.40-10.00 Uhr
Englische Nacherzählung (1 Stunde 20 Minuten Arbeitszeit)

The Miracle of the Messiah

One night in 1741 George Frederick Händel started out again of the aimless wonderings which had become a nightly habit. His mind was a battle-ground between hope, based on his past glories, and despair for the future. For 40 years he had written stately music, and kings and queens had showered him with honours. Then court society turned against him; jealous rivals hired rowdies to break up the performances of his operas. Handel was reduced to poverty. As he sank deeper and deeper into debt, he lost his creative spark. Nearing 60, he felt old and tired.

Hopelessly he returned to his shabby lodgings on that night. Entering, he saw a parcel on his desk. He broke the seal and tore off the wrapping. It was a libretto "A Oratorio", from a second-rate poet, Charles Jennens.

Handel grunted. Did Jennens dare to think he was inspired by God? Why hadn't he sent him an opera instead of this religious stuff ?

Absentmindedly he turned over page after page of the oratorio and some passages caught his eye: "He was despised and rejected of men . . . He looked for someone to have pity on him, but there was no man . . ." With a growing sense of kinship, Handel read on. "He trusted in God . . . God did not leave his soul in Hell ...Rejoice . . . Hallelujah".

The words began to glow with meaning, and he could feel the old fire rekindling. In his mind wondrous melodies tumbled over one another. Grabbing a pen, he started writing. With incredible swiftness the notes filled page after page.

For 24 days Handel laboured like a madman. Then he fell on his bed exhausted. On his desk lay the score* of The Messiah - the greatest oratorio ever written.

Donnerstag, den 19. Juni, von 10.15-11.45 Uhr
Ubersetzung aus dem Englischen in das Deutsche (1½ Stunden Arbeitszeit)

Effects of learning a now language

It was justly said by the Emperor Charles the Fifth, that to learn a new language was to aquire a new soul. He who is acquainted only with the writers of his native tongue, is in perpetual danger of confounding what is accidental with what is essential, and of supposing that tastes and habits of thought, which belong only to his own age and country, are inseparable from the nature of man. Initiated into foreign literature, he finds that principles of politics and morals, directly contrary to those which he has hitherto supposed to be unquestionable, because he never heard them questioned, have been held by large and enlightened communities; that feelings, which are so universal among his contemporaries, that he had supposed them instinctive, have been unknown to whole generations; that images, which have never failed to excite the ridicule of those among whom he has lived, have been thought sublime by millions. He thus loses that stupid contempt for everything beyond the wall of his celestial empire, which was the effect of his former ignorance. New associations take place among his ideas. He doubts where he formerly dogmatized. He tolerates where he formerly execrated. He ceases to confound that which is universal and eternal in human passions and opinions with that which is local and temporary. This is one of the most useful effects which results from studying the literature of other countries.

From "Thoughts on the Advancement of Academical Education in England" by Rose Macaulay.
